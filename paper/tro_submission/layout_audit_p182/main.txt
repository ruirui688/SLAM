Session-Level Map Admission Control for
Semantic-Segmentation-Assisted SLAM
in Dynamic Industrial Environments
Abstract—Long-term visual SLAM in industrial environments
faces a bottleneck that neither geometric SLAM nor objectlevel semantic mapping directly addresses: which detected objects
deserve durable map slots, and which should be rejected as
transient or dynamic contamination? Open-vocabulary detection pipelines produce candidate object instances from every
frame, but naively inserting all geometrically consistent detections contaminates the map with forklifts, carts, and singlevisit phantoms—a dynamic contamination problem recognized
in dynamic SLAM literature [1] but not resolved at the semantic
map-admission level. This paper formulates session-level map
admission control as a distinct contribution: an explicit objectmaintenance layer between perception and the map that transforms open-vocabulary RGB-D segmentation outputs into observation, tracklet, map-object, and revision layers, then applies
boolean criteria (multi-session presence, minimum observation
support, label consistency, static dominance) to admit stable
semantic landmarks and reject dynamic contamination. We
evaluate the framework on the TorWIC dataset [2], reporting a
reproducible evidence ladder across same-day (203 observations
/ 11 candidate clusters → 5 retained), cross-day (240/10→5),
and cross-month (297/14→7) Aisle protocols, with a separate
Hallway scene-transfer branch (537/16→9). Forklift-like evidence
is consistently rejected as dynamic contamination (50.0%–71.4%
rejection share across all four protocols). We validate the admission policy through parameter ablation across 27 combinations,
showing that min_sessions and min_frames are sensitive ﬁlters
while max_dynamic_ratio is naturally saturated by data bimodality (infrastructure at dynamic ratio 0.00, forklifts at ≥0.83).
Baseline comparison against naive-all-admit and purity/supportheuristic strategies demonstrates that simpler heuristics cannot
reject forklifts (phantom risk 20.0%→0.0% with the full policy,
McNemar p<0.001), while per-category retention analysis conﬁrms forklift rejection is universal and infrastructure retention
is selective but interpretable. A 16-conﬁguration DROID-SLAM
trajectory analysis reveals that selective low-coverage semantic
masks are trajectory-neutral (|∆APE| ≤ 0.006 mm, 11/16 conﬁgs
across 5 sessions and 2 scene types), while aggressive mask strategies introduce measurable perturbations (0.92–7.52 mm). The
contribution is bounded: not a lifelong SLAM benchmark, not
dense dynamic reconstruction, and not a downstream navigationgain claim—it is an evidence-backed session-level map admission
control methodology with per-object provenance at every layer.

Industrial environments amplify this question. Warehouses
and logistics centers contain stable infrastructure (barriers,
work tables, racks, pillars) alongside mobile agents (forklifts,
carts, human operators). An open-vocabulary detector such
as Grounding DINO [4] combined with a segmenter such
as SAM 2 [5] can propose bounding boxes and masks for
dozens of object instances per frame, but without an explicit admission-control policy, the map quickly accumulates
forklift-shaped phantom objects that are re-detected in revisits
but should never have been admitted in the ﬁrst place.
B. The Admission-Control Gap
Existing dynamic SLAM pipelines [1] are designed to mask,
track, and remove dynamic pixels and objects from the geometric reconstruction, ensuring that visual odometry and loop
closure operate on clean static geometry. Object-level SLAM
approaches [6]–[8] assign semantic identities to geometric
entities and can reason about object categories. However,
neither paradigm directly addresses the question of which
detected objects deserve a durable slot in the semantic map:
detection output is treated as fact once geometric consistency
is conﬁrmed, but in practice, repeated detections of forklifts in
roughly the same location can appear geometrically consistent
without being semantically admissible.
Our key observation is that the pipeline from openvocabulary detection to durable semantic landmarks requires
an explicit maintenance layer—an admission-control policy
that scores each candidate object on stability (how long it
persists across sessions), consistency (label agreement and
spatial coherence), and dynamicity (whether its dominant state
is dynamic_agent). This paper describes such a layer.

B. Dynamic SLAM and Dynamic-Object Suppression
Dynamic environments present a fundamental challenge
for SLAM. DynaSLAM [1] demonstrated that masking dynamic objects using Mask R-CNN and multi-view geometry improves camera tracking accuracy. Its follow-up, DynaSLAM II [13], added tightly-coupled multi-object tracking. Several systems target object-level dynamic handling:
Co-Fusion [14] segments and tracks multiple independentlymoving objects; MID-Fusion [15] uses an octree-based volumetric representation for multi-instance dynamic reconstruction; and FlowFusion [16] leverages optical ﬂow residuals for
dynamic/static segmentation.
DS-SLAM [17] combines SegNet with a movingconsistency check, while Detect-SLAM [18] makes object
detection and SLAM mutually beneﬁcial by using detected
keypoints only when objects are static. Saputra et al. [19]
provide a comprehensive survey of visual SLAM in dynamic
environments.
Relationship: Dynamic SLAM removes dynamic content to
protect geometric reconstruction (pose estimation and mapping). We address a different layer of the architecture: semantic map admission. An object may be correctly tracked as
dynamic (DynaSLAM’s goal) but still be erroneously admitted
to the long-term semantic map (our concern). The two layers
are complementary: dynamic SLAM cleans the geometric
map, while our maintenance layer cleans the semantic map.

D. Segmentation-Assisted Filtering
Recent advances in vision foundation models enable extracting semantically labeled object instances without task-speciﬁc
training. Grounding DINO [4] provides open-vocabulary
detection by marrying DINO with grounded pre-training.
SAM 2 [5] extends the Segment Anything paradigm to video
with promptable spatio-temporal masks. OpenCLIP [26] provides reproducible CLIP-scale vision-language embeddings
used for label reranking. At the 3D level, OpenMask3D [27]

Relationship: We treat these models as interchangeable
perception backends. Our maintenance layer consumes their
output (detected object instances with semantic labels and
masks) and applies session-level admission criteria. The architecture is backend-agnostic: any detection+segmentation
pipeline producing frame-level object instances can feed into
the maintenance layer.
IV. P ROBLEM F ORMULATION

The map-admission problem (motivated by the dynamiccontamination gap identiﬁed in §I-B) is: given candidate
observations across one or more sessions, produce a map M
consisting of durable object entities {e1 , . . . , eM } such that:
1) Stability: Each em corresponds to a physically persistent object in the environment (not a transient or
dynamic agent).
2) Consistency: The label and spatial extent of em are
consistent across revisits.
3) Completeness: No persistent object that appears across
multiple sessions with sufﬁcient evidence is omitted.
4) Admission control: Dynamic agents (forklifts, carts)
and transient objects are explicitly rejected rather than
silently suppressed.
B. Key Distinction from Standard SLAM
Standard SLAM treats map updates as: detection → geometric veriﬁcation → map insertion [3]. Our framework
inserts an intermediate maintenance layer: detection → observation (§V-A) → tracklet within-session (§V-B) → mapobject cross-session (§V-C) → revisioned map update (§V-F).
Each transition applies criteria that ﬁlter, aggregate, and score
candidate evidence before map insertion. This architecture
separates the perception question (“what is detected?”) from
the maintenance question (“what deserves a durable slot in the
semantic map?”).
V. M ETHOD : S ESSION -L EVEL M AP A DMISSION C ONTROL
Fig. 1 illustrates the full pipeline: open-vocabulary RGBD masks ﬂow through observation, tracklet, map-object, and
revision layers rather than being inserted directly into the map.
A. Observation Layer
For each RGB-D frame, we apply an open-vocabulary
detection pipeline:
1) Grounding DINO [4] proposes bounding boxes from
text prompts corresponding to common industrial object
categories (barrier, forklift, work table, warehouse rack,
cart, pallet, pillar).
2) SAM 2 [5] generates instance masks from the proposed
boxes.
3) OpenCLIP [26] reranks and resolves label assignments
by comparing mask crops to text embeddings of the
target category set.
Each resulting candidate becomes an ObjectObservation with
frame ID, session ID, bounding box, mask polygon, canonical
label, conﬁdence score, and detection timestamp. The detection pipeline is used as a black-box front end; our contribution
begins at the observation layer (§I-C).

A. Formal Statement
Let a SLAM session Sk produce a sequence of RGB-D
frames {Fk,1 , . . . , Fk,Nk }. For each frame, an open-vocabulary
detection-segmentation-labeling pipeline outputs a set of can(j)
didate object instances {ok,i }, each with a bounding box, a
segmentation mask, a canonical label, and a conﬁdence score.

B. Tracklet Layer
Within a single session Sk , observations with the same
canonical label and overlapping spatial extent are grouped into
tracklets. A tracklet T aggregates: session ID and temporal
extent (ﬁrst/last frame), observation count and spatial extent

4

statistics, label histogram (raw detector outputs before canonicalization), state histogram (detector-reported state: static,
candidate, dynamic_agent), and dynamic ratio ρT
(fraction of observations with state = dynamic_agent).
Tracklets are the within-session evidence unit; multiple tracklets with the same label from different sessions are candidates
for cross-session merging.
C. Map-Object Layer
Tracklets from different sessions are matched by spatial
overlap and canonical label consistency to form MapObjects.
A MapObject O aggregates: session support (number of distinct sessions), total observations and total tracklets, dominant
label and label purity (fraction of observations with the dominant label), dominant state, spatial stability (variance of mean
center across sessions), and meta-evidence (session lists, frame
counts, support counts). The cross-session matching uses a
spatial IoU threshold (0.1) with canonical label agreement.
Objects appearing in only one session with low support are
candidates for rejection.
D. Trust-Score Formulation

These criteria are the primary admission-control mechanism.
Objects that fail any criterion are recorded as rejected with
speciﬁc rejection reasons, providing auditable traceability (see
§VII-G for ablation and baseline comparison).
F. Revision Layer
When a new session revisits the environment, its tracklets
are matched against existing MapObjects in M. Three outcomes are possible:
1) Conﬁrm: A tracklet matches an existing MapObject
within spatial and label thresholds → increment session/support/observation counters, update statistics.
2) Add: A tracklet forms a new candidate MapObject
that passes admission criteria after sufﬁcient session
evidence → admit.
3) Reject: A tracklet does not match any existing object
and fails admission criteria → record as rejected with
reasons.
The map M is thus revisioned rather than rebuilt: stable
objects accumulate conﬁrming evidence across revisits, while
new candidates are admitted only after meeting the multisession threshold.

Each MapObject O receives a trust score τ (O) deﬁned as:
τ (O) = α · ssession (O) + β · ssupport (O) − γ · sdynamic (O) (1)
where ssession (O) is normalized session count (min 0, max 1
at ≥3 sessions), ssupport (O) is normalized observation support
(min 0, max 1 at ≥20 observations), sdynamic (O) = ρO is the
dynamic ratio (0 for fully static, 1 for fully dynamic), and
α = 0.4, β = 0.3, γ = 0.5 (conﬁgurable, not optimized; see
§VI-C for protocol-wide consistency).
This score is not presented as an optimal estimator. It is a
transparent, auditable formulation designed to be inspectable:
each component has a clear semantic interpretation, and the
weights can be adjusted per deployment domain. The score is
used as an auxiliary signal; the primary map-admission gate is
the boolean criteria set in §V-E. On the TorWIC evaluation set,
all retained MapObjects satisfy τ (O) ≥ 0.4 and all rejected
objects fall below τ (O) ≤ 0.15. These thresholds are observed
empirical properties of the current data and are not claimed
to generalize to other sites or frontend conﬁgurations without
recalibration.
E. Admission Criteria
A MapObject O is admitted to the durable map M if and
only if it satisﬁes all ﬁve criteria:
• Multi-session presence (≥ 2 sessions): single-session
observations may be transient.
• Minimum support (≥ 6 observations): low-support objects are likely spurious.
• Label consistency (label purity ≥ 0.7): mixed-label
objects indicate detector ambiguity.
• Static dominance (dynamic ratio ≤ 0.2): dynamic agents
should not enter the map.
• Minimum frames (≥ 4 frames): objects seen in only 1–2
frames are likely detection noise.

VI. E XPERIMENTAL P ROTOCOL
A. Dataset and Provenance
We use the TorWIC dataset from the POV-SLAM release [2], which provides RGB-D sequences of industrial
warehouse aisles with revisits under varying conditions. The
dataset contains 20 sessions across 3 dates (Jun 15, Jun 23,
Oct 12, 2022), totaling 32,743 RGB frames, with groundtruth camera trajectories and object annotations. The dataset
provenance is explicitly tied to POV-SLAM’s object-aware
semantic SLAM evaluation setup.
B. Protocol Design
We deﬁne four protocols spanning increasing temporal gaps
and a scene-transfer branch:
Primary Aisle Ladder. Three protocols using a “richer”
bundle conﬁguration covering a speciﬁc aisle section:
• Same-day: 4 sessions (Jun 15 Aisle CW/CCW runs), 203
observations, 11 candidate clusters, 5 retained MapObjects. Tests same-day viewpoint variation.
• Cross-day: 4 sessions (Jun 15 + Jun 23), 240 observations, 10 clusters, 5 retained. Tests 8-day revisit with
lighting change.
• Cross-month: 6 sessions (Jun 15, Jun 23, Oct 12), 297
observations, 14 clusters, 7 retained. Tests 4-month gap
with seasonal variation.
Hallway Scene-Transfer Branch. 10 sessions (10/10 complete) across all 3 dates, 537 observations, 16 clusters, 9
retained. Tests whether the admission-control framework transfers to a scene without forklifts (Hallway serve as a negativecontrol environment). Admission rate 56.2% (bootstrap 95%
CI: 31.2–81.2%, n=16); does not differ from pooled Aisle
(Fisher exact p=0.76).

D. Single-Site Limitation and Cross-Site Validation
The current evidence is limited to a single warehouse site
(TorWIC). While the Aisle and Hallway protocols provide
within-site protocol-level diversity, external validation at other
industrial sites (different warehouse layouts, lighting conditions, object categories) is deferred. A multi-site validation
would require: (1) a multi-site industrial SLAM dataset with
ground-truth object annotations across revisits (no such public
dataset exists to our knowledge); (2) recalibration of the detection prompt set for site-speciﬁc object categories; and (3) veriﬁcation that the admission thresholds (min_sessions=2,
max_dynamic_ratio=0.2) transfer without retuning. Until
such data is available, the current evidence is bounded to the
TorWIC environment, as noted among the limitations (§IX,
items 1–2).
IX. L IMITATIONS
1) Evaluation scale. The admission-control evaluation operates on 20 candidate clusters (across all protocols
after deduplication). Bootstrap 95% conﬁdence intervals
for per-protocol admission rates range from 18.2–72.7%
(same-day, n=11) to 31.2–81.2% (Hallway, n=16); the
pooled rate CI is 37.3–64.7% (n=51). Hallway versus
Aisle admission rates do not differ (Fisher exact p =
0.76). Between-baseline McNemar exact tests (Aisle
ladder, paired n=20): B0/B1 p<0.001, B1/B2 p=0.125
(n=4 discordant pairs), B0/B2 p<0.0001. B1/B2 is not
signiﬁcant at α=0.05 but the effect direction is deterministic (B2 never admits a cluster B1 rejects; all 4
discordant pairs are B1-admitted/B2-rejected forklifts).

Statistical power is limited by cluster count; conﬁdence
intervals and p-values should be interpreted with appropriate caution.
2) Single industrial site. TorWIC is collected in a single warehouse. Although Aisle and Hallway provide
protocol-level diversity, external validation at other industrial sites is deferred.
3) Detection conﬁdence not logged. Per-detection NN
conﬁdence scores were not preserved during observation
extraction. The label-purity proxy is used as a documented substitute.
4) Dynamic ratio may depend on frontend. The dynamic
ratio is computed from the detector’s state classiﬁer
(static / candidate / dynamic_agent). The observed bimodality (0.00 vs ≥0.83) may partially reﬂect the classiﬁer’s decision boundary rather than purely physical
separation. The insensitivity of max_dynamic_ratio to
threshold choice is demonstrated through ablation but
the bimodality’s dependence on frontend behavior is a
caveat.
5) No comparison against published map-management
policies. The baseline comparison (naive-all-admit, purity/support heuristic) uses deliberately simple policies
to isolate the contribution of individual signals. Comparison against map-management policies in published
SLAM systems (e.g., RTAB-Map’s memory management, ORB-SLAM3’s map-point culling) is deferred to
future work.
6) Single-session infrastructure loss. Infrastructure appearing in only one session cannot be veriﬁed for crosssession persistence and is conservatively rejected. This is
a design choice—precision over recall for map entries—
but entails genuine infrastructure omission.
7) Dynamic SLAM boundary. evo APE evaluation across
the bounded DROID-SLAM chain reveals three regimes.
Selective low-coverage semantic masks are trajectoryneutral (|∆APE| ≤ 0.006 mm in the original 7/12
neutral conﬁgs, extended by four neutral replication
sessions). Aggressive mask strategies (temporal/ﬂow
propagation, uniform ﬁrst-N masks) introduce measurable perturbations (0.92–7.52 mm, 5 conﬁgs). A highcoverage but still selective Jun 15 Aisle_CCW pressure
test (max 17.32% frame coverage, mean 0.568%) produces only a small perturbation (∆APE = +0.114 mm).
Mask selectivity is therefore necessary, but high dynamic
coverage can still move the trajectory at sub-millimetre
scale. Bootstrap 95% CI for the neutral rate is 43.8–
87.5% (11/16 conﬁgs); the two-group ∆APE separation
at |∆APE| ≤ 0.006 mm is complete (gap=0.914 mm, no
overlap). We do not claim navigation beneﬁt or full-map
quality improvement from masking.
8) Single VO backend. Only DROID-SLAM was tested as
the visual odometry backend. ORB-SLAM3 and other
backends may exhibit different sensitivity to dynamic
content masking.
9) Ground-truth object annotations. TorWIC provides
ground-truth object IDs; we use them for protocol
conﬁguration but the framework itself operates without

ground truth.
X. C ONCLUSION
This paper presents a systematic, evidence-backed approach to session-level map admission control for semanticsegmentation-assisted SLAM in dynamic industrial environments. The framework transforms open-vocabulary RGB-D
segmentation outputs into observation, tracklet, map-object,
and revision layers, then applies explicit persistence, stability,
and dynamicity signals to retain stable semantic landmarks
and suppress dynamic contamination.
Current TorWIC evidence documents a reproducible evidence chain across same-day, cross-day, and cross-month
Aisle protocols, with Hallway retained as a separate scenetransfer branch. Forklift-like evidence is consistently rejected
as dynamic contamination (50%–71% rejection share), while
barriers, work tables, and warehouse racks are selectively
retained based on multi-session evidence, label consistency,
and static dominance.
The dynamic SLAM backend evaluation identiﬁes a
boundary condition: selective low-coverage semantic masks
are trajectory-neutral for DROID-SLAM across the original
single-session sweep and four replication sessions, while aggressive mask strategies (propagation, uniform ﬁrst-N) introduce measurable perturbations (0.92–7.52 mm). A highcoverage selective-mask pressure test remains far below the
aggressive-mask failures but produces a small nonzero shift
(∆APE = +0.114 mm), showing that both selectivity and
coverage magnitude matter for bundle adjustment. The admission criteria are validated through parameter ablation across 27
parameter combinations, baseline comparison (demonstrating
that purity/support heuristics fail to reject any forklift), and
per-category retention/rejection analysis.
The contribution is bounded: not a lifelong SLAM benchmark, not dense dynamic reconstruction, and not a downstream
navigation-gain claim. It is a systematic map maintenance
methodology — ﬁve boolean criteria with per-object provenance at every layer — supported by reproducible evidence
and a complete supplementary evidence package that enables
per-cluster auditability of all admission and rejection decisions.
