\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% === I.A. Motivation ===
\subsection{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}

Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) has matured to the point where short-horizon geometric maps can be reliably constructed in real time~\cite{cadena2016past}. The next frontier---long-term SLAM and map reuse across weeks or months---shifts the burden from geometric consistency to semantic persistence: which detected objects should enter the map, and which should be discarded as transient or dynamic contamination?

Industrial environments amplify this question. Warehouses and logistics centers contain stable infrastructure (barriers, work tables, racks, pillars) alongside mobile agents (forklifts, carts, human operators). An open-vocabulary detector such as Grounding DINO~\cite{liu2024grounding} combined with a segmenter such as SAM~2~\cite{ravi2024sam2} can propose bounding boxes and masks for dozens of object instances per frame, but without an explicit admission-control policy, the map quickly accumulates forklift-shaped phantom objects that are re-detected in revisits but should never have been admitted in the first place.

% === I.B. The Admission-Control Gap ===
\subsection{The Admission-Control Gap}
\label{sec:admission_gap}

Existing dynamic SLAM pipelines~\cite{bescos2018dynaslam} are designed to mask, track, and remove dynamic pixels and objects from the geometric reconstruction, ensuring that visual odometry and loop closure operate on clean static geometry. Object-level SLAM approaches~\cite{yang2019cubeslam,peng2023openscene,jatavallabhula2023conceptfusion} assign semantic identities to geometric entities and can reason about object categories. However, neither paradigm directly addresses the question of which detected objects deserve a durable slot in the semantic map: detection output is treated as fact once geometric consistency is confirmed, but in practice, repeated detections of forklifts in roughly the same location can appear geometrically consistent without being semantically admissible.

Our key observation is that the pipeline from open-vocabulary detection to durable semantic landmarks requires an explicit maintenance layer---an admission-control policy that scores each candidate object on stability (how long it persists across sessions), consistency (label agreement and spatial coherence), and dynamicity (whether its dominant state is \texttt{dynamic\_agent}). This paper describes such a layer.

% === I.C. Scope and Contribution Boundaries ===
\subsection{Scope and Contribution Boundaries}
\label{sec:scope}

We deliberately limit the contribution to stable-object retention, dynamic-contamination rejection, and map-admission selectivity in industrial revisits. We do not claim to solve lifelong SLAM, dense dynamic reconstruction, or downstream navigation gain. The paper offers an architecturally auditable bridge from perception to map maintenance: each admission or rejection decision is traceable to per-object provenance (session counts, frame observations, label histograms, dynamicity scores), with reproducible evidence on the TorWIC dataset. This auditability is an architectural property, not a downstream-evaluated claim.

% II. CONTRIBUTIONS
% ============================================================================
\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{An explicit object-maintenance architecture} (Section~\ref{sec:method}) with observation, tracklet, map-object, and revision layers that separates detection from map admission. Each layer carries per-object provenance---frame counts, session IDs, label histograms, and dynamicity scores (Section~\ref{sec:trust_score})---enabling complete traceability of every admission or rejection decision to its underlying evidence.
  
  \item \textbf{A reproducible evidence ladder} (Section~\ref{sec:results_aisle}) on the TorWIC dataset~\cite{barath2023povslam} across three Aisle protocols (same-day, cross-day, cross-month) and a Hallway broader-validation branch (Section~\ref{sec:results_hallway}), all reported with invariant selection criteria and per-cluster-ID provenance.
  
  \item \textbf{A rejection-profile analysis} (Section~\ref{sec:results_rejection}) showing that forklift-like dynamic contamination is the primary rejection driver (50.0\%--71.4\% share) across all four protocols. Critically, forklifts achieve high label purity ($82\%$--$91\%$) and high observation counts (15--28 frames), so purity- or support-based heuristics alone cannot reject them.
  
  \item \textbf{Admission-criteria defense} (Section~\ref{sec:results_defense}): parameter ablation across 27 combinations confirming that min\_sessions and min\_frames are sensitive filters, while max\_dynamic\_ratio is insensitive across a wide range (0.10--0.30) due to natural separation of infrastructure (dynamic ratio $=$ 0.00) and forklifts ($\geq 0.83$) in the TorWIC data; baseline comparison demonstrating that purity/support heuristics cannot reject forklifts (phantom risk 21.1\% vs 0.0\% for the full policy).
  
  \item \textbf{Per-category retention and rejection analysis} (Section~\ref{sec:results_defense}): forklift rejection is universal (0/4 retained), infrastructure retention is selective and interpretable---barriers 40\%, work tables 50\%, racks 33\%---with each rejection attributable to one or more documented criteria.
  
  \item \textbf{A dynamic masking trajectory analysis} (Section~\ref{sec:results_dynamic_chain}): 12 DROID-SLAM configurations reveal two groups: selective semantic frontend masks and concentrated forklift masks produce trajectory-neutral results ($|\Delta\text{APE}| \leq 0.006~\text{mm}$, 7/12 configs), while aggressive mask strategies (temporal/flow propagation, uniform first-N masks) introduce measurable trajectory perturbations ($0.92$--$7.52~\text{mm}$, 5/12 configs), establishing that mask selectivity is a necessary condition for trajectory-neutrality in DROID-SLAM.
  
  \item \textbf{A modular, backend-agnostic maintenance layer} designed to consume output from any detection+segmentation pipeline. The trust-score and admission-criteria formulations are defined with configurable weights and semantics, enabling domain-specific tuning without modifying the underlying architecture (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

% === III.A ===
\subsection{Semantic SLAM and Object-Level Mapping}
\label{sec:related_semantic}

Semantic SLAM has progressed from geometric-only maps to systems that assign meaning to spatial entities. Cadena et al.~\cite{cadena2016past} survey the SLAM landscape from geometric to semantic, establishing that map reuse requires semantic understanding. Early object-level approaches---CubeSLAM~\cite{yang2019cubeslam} (cuboids in monocular SLAM) and MaskFusion~\cite{runz2018maskfusion} (instance-level RGB-D tracking)---demonstrated that object identity improves robustness and interpretability. Kimera~\cite{rosinol2020kimera} provides a complete metric-semantic SLAM pipeline with mesh reconstruction and semantic labeling, while SemanticFusion~\cite{mccormac2017semanticfusion} enabled dense 3D semantic maps from CNN predictions.

The open-vocabulary era has further expanded semantic mapping capabilities. OpenScene~\cite{peng2023openscene} demonstrated that language-conditioned 3D feature fields carry concept-level semantics without closed-set training. ConceptFusion~\cite{jatavallabhula2023conceptfusion} fused multiple foundation model outputs into an open-set 3D map. LERF~\cite{kerr2023lerf} grounded CLIP embeddings within neural radiance fields, enabling open-vocabulary 3D queries. These systems share a common architectural assumption: once an object is detected, it is admissible to the map. Our work challenges this assumption by introducing an explicit admission-control policy informed by cross-session stability.

\textit{Relationship:} We build on semantic SLAM's object-level representations but focus on a complementary problem: which detected objects deserve permanent map representation? A forklift detected with 0.95 confidence across five sessions is still a forklift---semantic confidence alone does not imply semantic admissibility.

% === III.B ===
\subsection{Dynamic SLAM and Dynamic-Object Suppression}
\label{sec:related_dynamic}

Dynamic environments present a fundamental challenge for SLAM. DynaSLAM~\cite{bescos2018dynaslam} demonstrated that masking dynamic objects using Mask R-CNN and multi-view geometry improves camera tracking accuracy. Its follow-up, DynaSLAM~II~\cite{bescos2021dynaslam2}, added tightly-coupled multi-object tracking. Several systems target object-level dynamic handling: Co-Fusion~\cite{runz2017cofusion} segments and tracks multiple independently-moving objects; MID-Fusion~\cite{xu2019midfusion} uses an octree-based volumetric representation for multi-instance dynamic reconstruction; and FlowFusion~\cite{zhang2020flowfusion} leverages optical flow residuals for dynamic/static segmentation.

DS-SLAM~\cite{yu2018dsslam} combines SegNet with a moving-consistency check, while Detect-SLAM~\cite{zhong2018detectslam} makes object detection and SLAM mutually beneficial by using detected keypoints only when objects are static. Saputra et al.~\cite{saputra2018visualslam} provide a comprehensive survey of visual SLAM in dynamic environments.

\textit{Relationship:} Dynamic SLAM removes dynamic content to protect geometric reconstruction (pose estimation and mapping). We address a different layer of the architecture: semantic map admission. An object may be correctly tracked as dynamic (DynaSLAM's goal) but still be erroneously admitted to the long-term semantic map (our concern). The two layers are complementary: dynamic SLAM cleans the geometric map, while our maintenance layer cleans the semantic map.

% === III.C ===
\subsection{Long-Term Map Maintenance}
\label{sec:related_longterm}

Long-term SLAM requires managing map memory across extended durations. RTAB-Map~\cite{labbe2019rtabmap} implements an appearance-based memory management system for large-scale and long-term operation. Churchill and Newman~\cite{churchill2012practice} demonstrated experience-based navigation where the robot maintains multiple map representations for different temporal conditions. Fehr et al.~\cite{fehr2016tsdf} proposed TSDF-based change detection for long-term dense reconstruction, distinguishing persistent structure from dynamic objects. ORB-SLAM3~\cite{campos2021orb3} introduced multi-map capabilities that enable map reuse and merging across sessions. The standard SLAM backends---ORB-SLAM2~\cite{murartal2015orb2} and DROID-SLAM~\cite{teed2021droidslam}---provide the visual odometry foundation on which our maintenance layer operates. POV-SLAM~\cite{barath2023povslam} introduced object-aware semantic SLAM and released the TorWIC dataset with ground-truth object annotations across multiple revisits.

\textit{Relationship:} Long-term mapping systems focus on memory management and geometric consistency across time. Our maintenance layer adds an orthogonal dimension: semantic admission control based on stability, persistence, and dynamicity signals---not just geometric or memory constraints.

The broader question of which landmarks to retain has been studied in classical SLAM under different names: EKF-SLAM feature management, PTAM map-point culling, and ORB-SLAM's map-point policies all implement observation-quality thresholds. These are geometric filters; our work is the first to carry this principle into semantic object-level map maintenance with explicit cross-session and dynamicity criteria.

% === III.D ===
\subsection{Segmentation-Assisted Filtering}
\label{sec:related_segmentation}

Recent advances in vision foundation models enable extracting semantically labeled object instances without task-specific training. Grounding~DINO~\cite{liu2024grounding} provides open-vocabulary detection by marrying DINO with grounded pre-training. SAM~2~\cite{ravi2024sam2} extends the Segment Anything paradigm to video with promptable spatio-temporal masks. OpenCLIP~\cite{cherti2023reproducible} provides reproducible CLIP-scale vision-language embeddings used for label reranking. At the 3D level, OpenMask3D~\cite{takmaz2023openmask3d} and CLIP-Fields~\cite{shafiullah2023clipfields} enable open-vocabulary 3D instance segmentation and semantic field learning, respectively---demonstrating that language-conditioned reasoning can operate directly on geometric representations.

\textit{Relationship:} We treat these models as interchangeable perception backends. Our maintenance layer consumes their output (detected object instances with semantic labels and masks) and applies session-level admission criteria. The architecture is backend-agnostic: any detection+segmentation pipeline producing frame-level object instances can feed into the maintenance layer.

% IV. PROBLEM FORMULATION
% ============================================================================
\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Formal Statement}
\label{sec:problem_formal}

Let a SLAM session $S_k$ produce a sequence of RGB-D frames $\{F_{k,1}, \ldots, F_{k,N_k}\}$. For each frame, an open-vocabulary detection-segmentation-labeling pipeline outputs a set of candidate object instances $\{o_{k,i}^{(j)}\}$, each with a bounding box, a segmentation mask, a canonical label, and a confidence score.

The \textbf{map-admission problem} (motivated by the dynamic-contamination gap identified in \S\ref{sec:admission_gap}) is: given candidate observations across one or more sessions, produce a map $\mathcal{M}$ consisting of durable object entities $\{e_1, \ldots, e_M\}$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Stability:} Each $e_m$ corresponds to a physically persistent object in the environment (not a transient or dynamic agent).
  \item \textbf{Consistency:} The label and spatial extent of $e_m$ are consistent across revisits.
  \item \textbf{Completeness:} No persistent object that appears across multiple sessions with sufficient evidence is omitted.
  \item \textbf{Admission control:} Dynamic agents (forklifts, carts) and transient objects are explicitly rejected rather than silently suppressed.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Distinction from Standard SLAM}
\label{sec:problem_distinction}

Standard SLAM treats map updates as: detection $\rightarrow$ geometric verification $\rightarrow$ map insertion~\cite{cadena2016past}. Our framework inserts an intermediate maintenance layer: detection $\rightarrow$ observation (\S\ref{sec:observation}) $\rightarrow$ tracklet within-session (\S\ref{sec:tracklet}) $\rightarrow$ map-object cross-session (\S\ref{sec:map_object}) $\rightarrow$ revisioned map update (\S\ref{sec:revision}). Each transition applies criteria that filter, aggregate, and score candidate evidence before map insertion. This architecture separates the perception question (``what is detected?'') from the maintenance question (``what deserves a durable slot in the semantic map?'').

% V. METHOD
% ============================================================================
\section{Method: Session-Level Map Admission Control}
\label{sec:method}

Fig.~1 illustrates the full pipeline: open-vocabulary RGB-D masks flow through observation, tracklet, map-object, and revision layers rather than being inserted directly into the map.

% === V.A Observation Layer ===
\subsection{Observation Layer}
\label{sec:observation}

For each RGB-D frame, we apply an open-vocabulary detection pipeline:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Grounding DINO}~\cite{liu2024grounding} proposes bounding boxes from text prompts corresponding to common industrial object categories (barrier, forklift, work table, warehouse rack, cart, pallet, pillar).
  \item \textbf{SAM~2}~\cite{ravi2024sam2} generates instance masks from the proposed boxes.
  \item \textbf{OpenCLIP}~\cite{cherti2023reproducible} reranks and resolves label assignments by comparing mask crops to text embeddings of the target category set.
\end{enumerate}
Each resulting candidate becomes an \textbf{ObjectObservation} with frame ID, session ID, bounding box, mask polygon, canonical label, confidence score, and detection timestamp. The detection pipeline is used as a black-box front end; our contribution begins at the observation layer (\S\ref{sec:scope}).

% === V.B Tracklet Layer ===
\subsection{Tracklet Layer}
\label{sec:tracklet}

Within a single session $S_k$, observations with the same canonical label and overlapping spatial extent are grouped into \textbf{tracklets}. A tracklet $T$ aggregates: session ID and temporal extent (first/last frame), observation count and spatial extent statistics, label histogram (raw detector outputs before canonicalization), state histogram (detector-reported state: \texttt{static}, \texttt{candidate}, \texttt{dynamic\_agent}), and dynamic ratio $\rho_T$ (fraction of observations with state $=$ \texttt{dynamic\_agent}). Tracklets are the within-session evidence unit; multiple tracklets with the same label from different sessions are candidates for cross-session merging.

% === V.C Map-Object Layer ===
\subsection{Map-Object Layer}
\label{sec:map_object}

Tracklets from different sessions are matched by spatial overlap and canonical label consistency to form \textbf{MapObjects}. A MapObject $O$ aggregates: session support (number of distinct sessions), total observations and total tracklets, dominant label and label purity (fraction of observations with the dominant label), dominant state, spatial stability (variance of mean center across sessions), and meta-evidence (session lists, frame counts, support counts). The cross-session matching uses a spatial IoU threshold (0.1) with canonical label agreement. Objects appearing in only one session with low support are candidates for rejection.

% === V.D Trust Score Formulation ===
\subsection{Trust-Score Formulation}
\label{sec:trust_score}

Each MapObject $O$ receives a \textbf{trust score} $\tau(O)$ defined as:
\begin{equation}
\tau(O) = \alpha \cdot s_{\text{session}}(O) + \beta \cdot s_{\text{support}}(O) - \gamma \cdot s_{\text{dynamic}}(O)
\label{eq:trust_score}
\end{equation}
where $s_{\text{session}}(O)$ is normalized session count (min~0, max~1 at $\geq$3 sessions), $s_{\text{support}}(O)$ is normalized observation support (min~0, max~1 at $\geq$20 observations), $s_{\text{dynamic}}(O) = \rho_O$ is the dynamic ratio (0 for fully static, 1 for fully dynamic), and $\alpha = 0.4$, $\beta = 0.3$, $\gamma = 0.5$ (configurable, not optimized; see \S\ref{sec:protocol_criteria} for protocol-wide consistency).

This score is \textbf{not} presented as an optimal estimator. It is a transparent, auditable formulation designed to be inspectable: each component has a clear semantic interpretation, and the weights can be adjusted per deployment domain. The score is used as an auxiliary signal; the primary map-admission gate is the boolean criteria set in \S\ref{sec:admission_criteria}. On the TorWIC evaluation set, all retained MapObjects satisfy $\tau(O) \geq 0.4$ and all rejected objects fall below $\tau(O) \leq 0.15$. These thresholds are observed empirical properties of the current data and are not claimed to generalize to other sites or frontend configurations without recalibration.

% === V.E Admission Criteria ===
\subsection{Admission Criteria}
\label{sec:admission_criteria}

A MapObject $O$ is admitted to the durable map $\mathcal{M}$ if and only if it satisfies \textbf{all} five criteria:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Multi-session presence} ($\geq$ 2 sessions): single-session observations may be transient.
  \item \textbf{Minimum support} ($\geq$ 6 observations): low-support objects are likely spurious.
  \item \textbf{Label consistency} (label purity $\geq$ 0.7): mixed-label objects indicate detector ambiguity.
  \item \textbf{Static dominance} (dynamic ratio $\leq$ 0.2): dynamic agents should not enter the map.
  \item \textbf{Minimum frames} ($\geq$ 4 frames): objects seen in only 1--2 frames are likely detection noise.
\end{itemize}
These criteria are the primary admission-control mechanism. Objects that fail any criterion are recorded as \textbf{rejected} with specific rejection reasons, providing auditable traceability (see \S\ref{sec:results_defense} for ablation and baseline comparison).

% === V.F Revision Layer ===
\subsection{Revision Layer}
\label{sec:revision}

When a new session revisits the environment, its tracklets are matched against existing MapObjects in $\mathcal{M}$. Three outcomes are possible:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Confirm:} A tracklet matches an existing MapObject within spatial and label thresholds $\rightarrow$ increment session/support/observation counters, update statistics.
  \item \textbf{Add:} A tracklet forms a new candidate MapObject that passes admission criteria after sufficient session evidence $\rightarrow$ admit.
  \item \textbf{Reject:} A tracklet does not match any existing object and fails admission criteria $\rightarrow$ record as rejected with reasons.
\end{enumerate}
The map $\mathcal{M}$ is thus \textbf{revisioned} rather than rebuilt: stable objects accumulate confirming evidence across revisits, while new candidates are admitted only after meeting the multi-session threshold.

\section{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

\subsection{Dataset and Provenance}
\label{sec:protocol_dataset}

We use the \textbf{TorWIC dataset} from the POV-SLAM release~\cite{barath2023povslam}, which provides RGB-D sequences of industrial warehouse aisles with revisits under varying conditions. The dataset contains 20 sessions across 3 dates (Jun~15, Jun~23, Oct~12, 2022), totaling 32,743 RGB frames, with ground-truth camera trajectories and object annotations. The dataset provenance is explicitly tied to POV-SLAM's object-aware semantic SLAM evaluation setup.

\subsection{Protocol Design}
\label{sec:protocol_design}

We define four protocols spanning increasing temporal gaps and a scene-transfer branch:

\textbf{Primary Aisle Ladder.} Three protocols using a ``richer'' bundle configuration covering a specific aisle section:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Same-day:} 4 sessions (Jun~15 Aisle CW/CCW runs), 203 observations, 11 candidate clusters, 5 retained MapObjects. Tests same-day viewpoint variation.
  \item \textbf{Cross-day:} 4 sessions (Jun~15 + Jun~23), 240 observations, 10 clusters, 5 retained. Tests 8-day revisit with lighting change.
  \item \textbf{Cross-month:} 6 sessions (Jun~15, Jun~23, Oct~12), 297 observations, 14 clusters, 7 retained. Tests 4-month gap with seasonal variation.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Hallway Scene-Transfer Branch.} 10 sessions (10/10 complete) across all 3 dates, 537 observations, 16 clusters, 9 retained. Tests whether the admission-control framework transfers to a scene without forklifts (Hallway serve as a negative-control environment). Admission rate 56.2\% (bootstrap 95\% CI: 31.2--81.2\%, n=16); does not differ from pooled Aisle (Fisher exact p=0.76).

\subsection{Selection Criteria (Constant Across All Protocols)}
\label{sec:protocol_criteria}

All results are reported with invariant selection criteria: \texttt{min\_sessions}=2, \texttt{min\_frames}=4, \texttt{min\_support}=6, \texttt{max\_dynamic\_ratio}=0.2, \texttt{min\_label\_purity}=0.7. These values are fixed before evaluation and not tuned per protocol. Parameter sensitivity is explored through ablation across 27 combinations (\S\ref{sec:results_defense}).

\subsection{Dynamic SLAM: Mask Coverage and Trajectory Analysis}
\label{sec:results_dynamic_chain}

We evaluate 12 DROID-SLAM configurations on a single 64-frame TorWIC window (Jun~15 Aisle\_CW\_Run\_1), comparing raw RGB against masked RGB inputs. The ground-truth trajectory (TUM format) is from the TorWIC dataset; all metrics are computed with \texttt{evo\_ape} and \texttt{evo\_rpe} (translation part, SE(3) Umeyama alignment). The results reveal two distinct groups:

\textbf{Group~1: Trajectory-Neutral (7/12 configs).} Configurations using no masking (P132, P134), our semantic frontend masks (P135), or concentrated forklift-only masks (P142-T4/T8/T16) all produce $|\Delta\text{APE}| \leq 0.006~\text{mm}$, effectively tied with the raw baseline (APE RMSE $=$ 0.109855~m for 64-frame GBA, 0.001428~m for 8-frame P132). RPE is similarly invariant ($|\Delta\text{RPE}| \leq 0.027~\text{mm}$). P135 (semantic masks) performs identically to P142 (oracle concentrated masks), validating that our frontend produces sufficiently selective forklift masks.

\textbf{Group~2: Perturbed (5/12 configs).} Configurations using aggressive mask strategies produce measurable trajectory perturbations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item P136 (temporal propagation stress) and P137 (flow propagation stress): $\Delta\text{APE} = +7.517~\text{mm}$. Propagation masks extend beyond actual forklift frames, removing visual features from frames that DROID-SLAM requires for bundle adjustment. The identical magnitude across both propagation methods ($\pm 0~\text{mm}$) suggests mask \textit{strategy} (which frames are masked) determines perturbation magnitude, not the propagation \textit{method}.
  \item P138--P140 (first-8/16/32 uniform masks): $\Delta\text{APE} \approx +0.92~\text{mm}$. Blindly masking the first $N$ frames depletes DROID-SLAM's initialization window. The magnitude plateaus after 8 frames ($0.922 \rightarrow 0.921 \rightarrow 0.920~\text{mm}$ for 8/16/32 frames), confirming that initialization, not cumulative frame masking, is the vulnerable phase.
\end{itemize}

These results establish two findings. First, semantic frontend masks that are selective (only actual forklift frames) are trajectory-neutral for DROID-SLAM bundle adjustment---our method achieves this condition. Second, mask selectivity is a non-trivial requirement: aggressive mask strategies that propagate beyond true dynamic regions or blindly mask initialization frames introduce measurable trajectory degradation. The detailed per-config metrics are provided in the supplementary material (Table~S4).

Stage~1 replication on two additional TorWIC Aisle sessions confirms cross-session reproducibility: Jun~15 Aisle\_CW\_Run\_2 (same-day) and Jun~23 Aisle\_CW\_Run\_1 (cross-day, two weeks later) both produce $\Delta\text{APE} \leq 0.001~\text{mm}$ with selective semantic frontend masks (4/64 frames masked, max coverage $\leq 1.49\%$). Stage~2 extends this to cross-month and scene-transfer: Oct~12 Aisle\_CW (cross-month, four months later, $\Delta\text{APE}=0.000~\text{mm}$, 2/64 masked, max~$4.89\%$) and Oct~12 Hallway\_Full\_CW\_Run\_2 (first-ever Hallway dynamic mask test, $\Delta\text{APE}=0.000~\text{mm}$, 3/64 masked, max~$0.63\%$). This upgrades to an 11/16-config, 5-session evidence chain (same-day $+$ cross-day $+$ cross-month $+$ Hallway scene-transfer). A follow-up high-coverage selective-mask pressure test on Jun~15 Aisle\_CCW\_Run\_1 masks 5/64 frames with max per-frame coverage $17.32\%$ and mean coverage $0.568\%$; it remains small in absolute terms but no longer exactly neutral ($\Delta\text{APE}=+0.114~\text{mm}$, $\Delta\text{RPE}=-0.006~\text{mm}$). Thus, selectivity is necessary but not sufficient by itself: coverage magnitude also defines the boundary between trajectory-neutral and mildly perturbed behavior.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

% ===== Discussion content ported from manuscript_en_thick.md §VIII =====

\subsection{Why Boolean Criteria (Not Learned)}
\label{sec:disc_boolean}

The five admission criteria (\S\ref{sec:admission_criteria}) are deliberately boolean rather than learned. Three considerations motivate this choice.

\textbf{Traceability.} Every admission or rejection decision maps to a specific criterion violation, enabling per-object provenance auditable without model introspection. Learned classifiers (e.g., a neural admission gate trained on object features) would obscure the decision boundary.

\textbf{Cross-scene transfer.} Boolean thresholds on session count, label purity, and dynamic ratio transfer across the Aisle and Hallway protocols without retraining. A learned gate trained on Aisle data might specialize to forklift-rich scenes and fail on the Hallway negative-control environment.

\textbf{Domain-informed priorities.} The weights in the trust score (\ref{eq:trust_score}) reflect domain priorities---session count ($\alpha{=}0.4$) is weighted more heavily than observation support ($\beta{=}0.3$) because cross-session persistence is a stronger indicator of infrastructure than repeated detection within a single session---rather than being optimized on a validation set. The dynamic-ratio weight ($\gamma{=}0.5$) intentionally penalizes dynamic contamination to produce a clean floor: $\tau(O) \leq 0.15$ for forklifts, $\tau(O) \geq 0.4$ for infrastructure.

\subsection{Dynamic-Ratio Bimodality}
\label{sec:disc_bimodality}

The observed separation---infrastructure clusters at dynamic ratio $=$ 0.00, forklifts at $\geq$0.83, with no clusters in the intermediate range (0.01--0.82)---is a striking feature of the data. This bimodality justifies the insensitivity of \texttt{max\_dynamic\_ratio} across a wide range (0.10--0.30) shown in the parameter ablation (\S\ref{sec:results_defense}).

However, this bimodality may partially reflect the frontend state classifier's decision boundary rather than purely physical separation. The detector reports three states (\texttt{static}, \texttt{candidate}, \texttt{dynamic\_agent}); if the classifier assigns \texttt{dynamic\_agent} conservatively, dynamic objects receive consistently high ratios while static objects receive zero. This caveat is noted as a limitation (\S\ref{sec:limitations}, item 4): the bimodality is real in the data but its sharpness may be frontend-dependent. Multi-frontend ablation would be needed to quantify this dependence.

\subsection{Relationship to Landmark Selection in Classical SLAM}
\label{sec:disc_landmark}

The admission-control problem has conceptual precursors in classical SLAM. EKF-SLAM feature management, PTAM map-point culling, and ORB-SLAM's map-point policies all implement observation-quality thresholds for geometric landmarks. ORB-SLAM3's map-merging capability further introduces cross-session geometric verification. Our admission criteria carry this principle into the semantic domain: just as ORB-SLAM culls map points with low observation count or high reprojection error, our framework culls semantic objects with low session support or high dynamic ratio. The key difference is that semantic admission requires reasoning about object identity persistence rather than geometric reprojection error, and the criteria must distinguish between static infrastructure and structurally similar dynamic agents (forklifts that look geometrically stable but are semantically inadmissible). RTAB-Map's memory management similarly filters based on retrieval frequency; our session-count criterion is a semantic analogue.

\subsection{Single-Site Limitation and Cross-Site Validation}
\label{sec:disc_cross_site}

The current evidence is limited to a single warehouse site (TorWIC). While the Aisle and Hallway protocols provide within-site protocol-level diversity, external validation at other industrial sites (different warehouse layouts, lighting conditions, object categories) is deferred. A multi-site validation would require: (1)~a multi-site industrial SLAM dataset with ground-truth object annotations across revisits (no such public dataset exists to our knowledge); (2)~recalibration of the detection prompt set for site-specific object categories; and (3)~verification that the admission thresholds (\texttt{min\_sessions}{=}2, \texttt{max\_dynamic\_ratio}{=}0.2) transfer without retuning. Until such data is available, the current evidence is bounded to the TorWIC environment, as noted among the limitations (\S\ref{sec:limitations}, items~1--2).

% IX. LIMITATIONS

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Evaluation scale.} The admission-control evaluation operates on 20 candidate clusters (across all protocols after deduplication). Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for per-protocol admission rates range from 18.2--72.7\% (same-day, n=11) to 31.2--81.2\% (Hallway, n=16); the pooled rate CI is 37.3--64.7\% (n=51). Hallway versus Aisle admission rates do not differ (Fisher exact $p=0.76$). Between-baseline McNemar exact tests (Aisle ladder, paired n=20): B0/B1 $p{<}0.001$, B1/B2 $p{=}0.125$ (n=4 discordant pairs), B0/B2 $p{<}0.0001$. B1/B2 is not significant at $\alpha{=}0.05$ but the effect direction is deterministic (B2 never admits a cluster B1 rejects; all 4 discordant pairs are B1-admitted/B2-rejected forklifts). Statistical power is limited by cluster count; confidence intervals and p-values should be interpreted with appropriate caution.
  \item \textbf{Single industrial site.} TorWIC is collected in a single warehouse. Although Aisle and Hallway provide protocol-level diversity, external validation at other industrial sites is deferred.
  \item \textbf{Detection confidence not logged.} Per-detection NN confidence scores were not preserved during observation extraction. The label-purity proxy is used as a documented substitute.
  \item \textbf{Dynamic ratio may depend on frontend.} The dynamic ratio is computed from the detector's state classifier (static / candidate / dynamic\_agent). The observed bimodality (0.00 vs $\geq$0.83) may partially reflect the classifier's decision boundary rather than purely physical separation. The insensitivity of max\_dynamic\_ratio to threshold choice is demonstrated through ablation but the bimodality's dependence on frontend behavior is a caveat.
  \item \textbf{No comparison against published map-management policies.} The baseline comparison (naive-all-admit, purity/support heuristic) uses deliberately simple policies to isolate the contribution of individual signals. Comparison against map-management policies in published SLAM systems (e.g., RTAB-Map's memory management, ORB-SLAM3's map-point culling) is deferred to future work.
  \item \textbf{Single-session infrastructure loss.} Infrastructure appearing in only one session cannot be verified for cross-session persistence and is conservatively rejected. This is a design choice---precision over recall for map entries---but entails genuine infrastructure omission.
  \item \textbf{Dynamic SLAM boundary.} evo APE evaluation across the bounded DROID-SLAM chain reveals three regimes. Selective low-coverage semantic masks are trajectory-neutral ($|\Delta\text{APE}| \leq 0.006~\text{mm}$ in the original 7/12 neutral configs, extended by four neutral replication sessions). Aggressive mask strategies (temporal/flow propagation, uniform first-N masks) introduce measurable perturbations ($0.92$--$7.52~\text{mm}$, 5 configs). A high-coverage but still selective Jun~15 Aisle\_CCW pressure test (max $17.32\%$ frame coverage, mean $0.568\%$) produces only a small perturbation ($\Delta\text{APE}=+0.114~\text{mm}$). Mask selectivity is therefore necessary, but high dynamic coverage can still move the trajectory at sub-millimetre scale. Bootstrap 95\% CI for the neutral rate is 43.8--87.5\% (11/16 configs); the two-group $\Delta$APE separation at $|\Delta\text{APE}| \leq 0.006~\text{mm}$ is complete (gap=0.914~mm, no overlap). We do not claim navigation benefit or full-map quality improvement from masking.
  \item \textbf{Single VO backend.} Only DROID-SLAM was tested as the visual odometry backend. ORB-SLAM3 and other backends may exhibit different sensitivity to dynamic content masking.
  \item \textbf{Ground-truth object annotations.} TorWIC provides ground-truth object IDs; we use them for protocol configuration but the framework itself operates without ground truth.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents a systematic, evidence-backed approach to session-level map admission control for semantic-segmentation-assisted SLAM in dynamic industrial environments. The framework transforms open-vocabulary RGB-D segmentation outputs into observation, tracklet, map-object, and revision layers, then applies explicit persistence, stability, and dynamicity signals to retain stable semantic landmarks and suppress dynamic contamination.

Current TorWIC evidence documents a reproducible evidence chain across same-day, cross-day, and cross-month Aisle protocols, with Hallway retained as a separate scene-transfer branch. Forklift-like evidence is consistently rejected as dynamic contamination (50\%--71\% rejection share), while barriers, work tables, and warehouse racks are selectively retained based on multi-session evidence, label consistency, and static dominance.

The dynamic SLAM backend evaluation identifies a boundary condition: selective low-coverage semantic masks are trajectory-neutral for DROID-SLAM across the original single-session sweep and four replication sessions, while aggressive mask strategies (propagation, uniform first-N) introduce measurable perturbations ($0.92$--$7.52~\text{mm}$). A high-coverage selective-mask pressure test remains far below the aggressive-mask failures but produces a small nonzero shift ($\Delta\text{APE}=+0.114~\text{mm}$), showing that both selectivity and coverage magnitude matter for bundle adjustment. The admission criteria are validated through parameter ablation across 27 parameter combinations, baseline comparison (demonstrating that purity/support heuristics fail to reject any forklift), and per-category retention/rejection analysis.

The contribution is bounded: not a lifelong SLAM benchmark, not dense dynamic reconstruction, and not a downstream navigation-gain claim. It is a systematic map maintenance methodology --- five boolean criteria with per-object provenance at every layer --- supported by reproducible evidence and a complete supplementary evidence package that enables per-cluster auditability of all admission and rejection decisions.
